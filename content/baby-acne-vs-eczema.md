Baby acne and eczema can both affect the cheeks, but they behave differently. **Baby acne usually produces small bumps and often clears without treatment; eczema is typically dry, itchy and inflamed and may need a care plan.** A photograph cannot confirm every rash, so use these clues to decide what to discuss with the pediatrician—not to self-prescribe adult treatments.

## 🔍 Clue 1: Bumps or Dry, Itchy Patches?

Baby acne often appears as small inflamed bumps on the face and neck, commonly within the first several weeks after birth. Milia—tiny white facial bumps—can appear at the same time but are different and also usually resolve on their own.

Eczema can look dry, rough, scaly or swollen. On lighter skin it may look pink or red; on darker skin it can look purple, gray, brown or darker than surrounding skin. Itch is an important clue: a baby may rub the face, seem uncomfortable or sleep poorly.

## 📍 Clue 2: Timing and Location

Mayo Clinic notes that baby acne often develops around two to four weeks and commonly affects the cheeks, forehead, neck, chest or back. It is temporary and usually does not scar.

Baby eczema frequently affects the cheeks and scalp, then can involve arms, legs or other areas. Saliva, sweat, dry air, fragrance and rough fabrics can worsen it. Crusting or oozing does not automatically mean eczema and needs assessment for infection.

## 🫧 What to Do While You Arrange Advice

Wash the face once daily with lukewarm water and, if needed, a tiny amount of mild fragrance-free cleanser. Pat dry. Do not squeeze bumps, scrub scales or use salicylic acid, benzoyl peroxide, retinoids, essential oils or toothpaste on a baby.

For suspected baby acne, adding products often causes more irritation. For diagnosed eczema, the AAD recommends short lukewarm baths and prompt use of fragrance-free cream or ointment, plus prescribed medicine exactly as directed.

## 📝 Track the Pattern

Take one well-lit photo daily in the same room. Note itch, sleep, feeding, new products, fever and where the rash spreads. Do not change food or formula because of a facial rash without medical advice; most childhood eczema does not have one obvious food cause.

## 🚩 When to Call Promptly

Contact the pediatrician for fever, blisters, pus, honey-colored crusts, rapidly spreading redness, pain, eye involvement, poor feeding or a baby who seems ill. Also arrange evaluation when the rash is very itchy, disrupts sleep, persists for months or does not improve with the recommended plan.

## ❓ Quick Questions

**Does breast milk treat baby acne?** It is not an established acne treatment and can leave moisture on the skin. Clean spit-up gently and follow pediatric advice.

**Will baby acne scar?** Typical neonatal acne usually clears without scarring; squeezing increases avoidable irritation.

**Can eczema have bumps?** Yes. Eczema can be bumpy, especially on darker skin, which is why itch, dryness and professional examination matter.

## ✅ Bottom Line

Baby acne is usually temporary and bump-focused; eczema is more likely to be dry and itchy. Keep care minimal and let a pediatric clinician identify uncertain, persistent or inflamed rashes.
