Rosacea and sensitive skin can both involve burning, stinging, dryness, or redness. The important difference is that **rosacea is a chronic medical condition**, while sensitive skin is a broad description of skin that reacts easily. You cannot confirm rosacea from an online checklist, but patterns can tell you when to see a dermatologist.

## 1. Look for Repeated Central-Face Flushing

Rosacea often affects the cheeks, nose, chin, or forehead. Flushing may initially come and go, then linger longer. On deeper skin tones, obvious redness can be subtle; warmth, burning, swelling, or color change may be easier to notice.

## 2. Check for Other Rosacea Clues

Visible small blood vessels, acne-like bumps without typical blackheads, thickening skin, or irritated watery eyes can occur. Sensitive skin alone is more likely to react directly after a particular product or exposure, though the two can exist together.

## 4. Build a Rosacea-Friendly Routine

Wash with fingertips and lukewarm water, then pat dry. Choose fragrance-free products and moisturize regularly. Patch test new skincare for seven to ten days. Avoid scrubbing, harsh toners, and repeatedly experimenting on a flare.

## 5. Make Sun Protection Daily

Sun is a common rosacea trigger. Use broad-spectrum SPF 30 or higher plus shade and a hat. Mineral formulas with zinc oxide or titanium dioxide may be less irritating for some people, but personal tolerance decides.

## 🩺 When to See a Dermatologist

Book an appointment for persistent flushing, recurring bumps, visible vessels, thickening skin, or symptoms that affect confidence and daily life. Seek prompt eye care for pain, light sensitivity, blurred vision, marked redness, or a gritty sensation. Effective prescription and procedural treatments exist; skincare alone may not control rosacea.

## ✅ Bottom Line

Sensitive skin describes reactivity; rosacea has recognizable recurring patterns and needs medical diagnosis. A gentle routine can reduce irritation for both, but persistent central-face flushing or eye symptoms should not be self-treated indefinitely.
