Rosacea and perioral dermatitis can both cause small facial bumps, discoloration, dryness and burning. **Rosacea usually centers on the cheeks and nose**, while perioral dermatitis commonly clusters around the mouth and may also appear near the nose or eyes.

Neither condition should be diagnosed from a photograph alone. Acne, eczema, contact dermatitis and other rashes can look similar, especially across different skin tones.

## 🌹 Typical Rosacea Clues

Rosacea may begin with easy flushing and warmth. Color can become persistent, and visible vessels or acne-like bumps may develop across the central face. Skin often stings with water or products. Some people also have gritty, dry, bloodshot or light-sensitive eyes.

Sun, heat, stress, alcohol and spicy foods are common—but not universal—flare triggers. Rosacea is chronic, so management often combines medication, gentle skincare, sunscreen and trigger tracking.

## 👄 Typical Perioral Dermatitis Clues

Perioral dermatitis forms groups of tiny bumps around an opening, most often the mouth. The skin immediately next to the lip border may be relatively spared. The rash may be red, pink, skin-colored or darker than surrounding skin, and can feel dry, flaky, itchy or burning.

It may extend around the nostrils or eyes, which is why “peri-orificial dermatitis” is a more complete name. Unlike rosacea, prominent flushing and facial vessels are not defining features.

## ⚠️ The Steroid-Cream Connection

Long or inappropriate use of topical corticosteroids, including over-the-counter hydrocortisone, is linked with many cases of perioral dermatitis. The rash can temporarily look better while steroid is used, then rebound when it stops.

Do not abruptly stop a prescribed steroid without contacting the prescriber. A clinician can guide withdrawal and decide whether antibiotic or another prescription treatment is needed. Never apply skin antibiotics near the eyes unless specifically prescribed for that location.

## 🫧 Simplify While Seeking Care

Use a mild, fragrance-free cleanser and fingertips. Avoid scrubs, peels, essential oils and multiple acne treatments. A complicated routine can sustain irritation and make the pattern harder to evaluate.

## ☀️ Use Gentle Sun Protection

Sun commonly triggers rosacea and can worsen discoloration after any rash. Try a fragrance-free mineral sunscreen on a small area first. Hats and shade reduce reliance on a product when facial skin is very reactive.

## 🩺 What a Dermatologist May Ask

Bring a list of prescription and over-the-counter creams, nasal or inhaled steroids, toothpaste, cosmetics and recent changes. Mention eye discomfort and whether heat causes flushing. Perioral dermatitis may clear over weeks or months after the correct plan; restarting steroid for temporary relief can prolong the cycle.

Clear photos taken before treatment can also help document the original pattern.

## ❓ Quick Questions

**Is perioral dermatitis contagious?** No.

**Can it look like acne?** Yes, but blackheads are not typical and the mouth-area distribution is a clue.

**Can I use hydrocortisone to calm it?** Do not self-treat a suspected case with steroid. Contact the prescriber before stopping prescription steroid.

## ✅ Bottom Line

Central flushing and visible vessels favor rosacea; a clustered mouth-area rash and steroid history favor perioral dermatitis. A clinician can distinguish them and choose the correct treatment.
