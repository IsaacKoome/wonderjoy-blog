Baby heat rash—also called prickly heat or miliaria—develops when tiny sweat-gland openings become blocked and sweat is trapped beneath the skin. It often looks like clusters of small bumps or tiny clear blisters on the neck, hairline, chest, back, folds or under clothing.

Heat rash should occur in an otherwise well baby. Fever, lethargy, pus or a rapidly worsening rash is not something to dismiss as “just heat.”

## ❄️ Cool First

Move the baby into a cooler, well-ventilated room. Remove extra layers and use a cool—not ice-cold—bath or damp compress to wash away sweat. Pat the skin fully dry and leave affected areas open to air when safe.

Use air conditioning or a fan to cool the room, but do not aim a strong cold stream directly at the baby's face. Offer usual feeds and watch hydration during hot weather.

## 👕 Dress for Airflow

Choose thin, loose-fitting cotton clothing. Check the back of the neck and chest rather than hands or feet when judging overheating. Car seats, carriers and swaddles can trap warmth, so take supervised breaks and follow safe-sleep guidance.

Clean damp folds—especially the neck, armpits and thighs—then dry gently. Milk and drool can add moisture and friction to the same areas.

## 🚫 Skip Heavy and Irritating Products

Oils, thick ointments and greasy hair products can block sweat openings and worsen heat rash in some areas. Avoid powder, essential oils, menthol, fragranced lotion and adult anti-itch or steroid creams unless the pediatrician recommends a specific product.

If surrounding skin is dry, ask where a light fragrance-free moisturizer can be used without covering the heat-rash bumps.

## ☀️ Prevent the Next Flare

Plan outdoor time for cooler hours, use shade and avoid direct sun. For infants younger than six months, shade and clothing are the first protection strategy; ask the pediatrician before sunscreen. Never cover a stroller with a blanket that blocks airflow and traps heat.

## 🚩 When to Call

Contact the pediatrician if cooling does not help within a few days, the rash is severe, spreads, becomes painful, or develops pus, warmth or swelling. Seek urgent advice for any fever in a young infant, poor feeding, fewer wet diapers, unusual sleepiness, breathing trouble or a baby who feels very hot.

## ❓ Quick Questions

**Can heat rash happen in winter?** Yes. Too many layers or an occlusive ointment can trap sweat even when it is cold outside.

**Should I pop the tiny blisters?** No. Keep the area cool and clean; opening the skin adds injury and infection risk.

**Is heat rash the same as eczema?** No. They can occur together, but eczema is generally persistently dry and itchy, while heat rash follows sweat and overheating.

## ✅ Bottom Line

Cool the baby, remove extra layers, dry damp folds and avoid heavy products. A well baby should improve; fever or infection signs require medical care.
