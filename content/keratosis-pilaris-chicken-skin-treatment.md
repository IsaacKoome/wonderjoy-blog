Tiny rough bumps on the upper arms, thighs, cheeks, or buttocks are often called “chicken skin.” The medical name is **keratosis pilaris (KP)**, a harmless condition caused by plugs of keratin and dead skin cells around hair follicles.

KP is not contagious, not caused by poor hygiene, and not ordinary acne. Treatment can make it smoother, but it tends to need maintenance rather than a one-time cure.

## 🔍 How Keratosis Pilaris Usually Looks

KP often feels like fine sandpaper or permanent goosebumps. The bumps may be skin-colored, red, brown, or darker than the surrounding skin, and dry weather can make them more noticeable. They usually appear in patches rather than as isolated painful pimples.

See a clinician if bumps are very itchy, spreading rapidly, filled with pus, or difficult to identify. Eczema, folliculitis, psoriasis, and other conditions can resemble rough follicular bumps.

## 1. Start With Moisture, Not Scrubbing

Dryness makes KP look and feel rougher. Take short lukewarm showers, use a mild fragrance-free wash, and pat rather than rub. Within about five minutes of getting out, apply a cream while the skin is still slightly damp.

Creams and ointments generally hold water better than thin lotions. Ingredients such as glycerin and ceramides support hydration; petrolatum can help very dry areas.

## 2. Consider a Keratolytic Ingredient

Keratolytics loosen the excess dead skin around the follicle. Dermatologists commonly use products containing **lactic acid, urea, salicylic acid, glycolic acid, or a retinoid**. You do not need all of them.

Apply one product as directed to a small test area first. Begin two or three nights weekly if the label permits, then adjust based on comfort. Stop for several days if the area becomes sore, shiny, cracked, or persistently red.

## 3. Avoid the “Scrub It Smooth” Trap

A rough loofah may make skin feel smoother for an hour, then trigger more irritation and discoloration. If you use a washcloth, keep pressure light and do not combine vigorous rubbing with an acid on the same day. Never exfoliate broken, sunburned, or inflamed skin.

## 4. Reduce Friction and Picking

Tight sleeves, repeated leaning, shaving, and picking can inflame follicular bumps. Use shaving cream, a sharp clean razor, and shave with hair growth if hair removal aggravates the area. Squeezing KP does not remove the underlying tendency and can leave marks.

## 5. Protect Exposed Areas

Sun does not eliminate KP, and irritation plus UV exposure can make discoloration more persistent. Use broad-spectrum SPF 30 or higher on uncovered arms and legs.

## ⏳ When Should You See Improvement?

The AAD suggests checking in with a dermatologist if a treatment plan has not improved KP after about **four to six weeks**. Even when texture improves, maintenance once or twice weekly may be needed. Results vary, and completely poreless skin is not a realistic goal.

## ❓ Quick Questions

**Is KP acne?** No. It involves keratin plugs around follicles, not the same acne process.

**Can I cure it permanently?** There is no guaranteed permanent cure, but many people control texture with consistent moisturizing and gentle keratolytics.

**Can darker skin use acids for KP?** Yes, but over-irritation can create dark marks. Start slowly, moisturize, and stop if inflammation develops.

## ✅ Bottom Line

Keratosis pilaris is harmless and common. Moisturize damp skin, add one gentle keratolytic if needed, avoid aggressive friction, and expect gradual management rather than an overnight transformation.
