Retinol can improve mild fine lines, uneven texture and pigmentation, and other retinoids can also treat acne. Yet menopause often makes skin drier and more reactive—the exact conditions that increase peeling and burning.

**Retinol is optional, not the price of admission to healthy aging.** If you use it, tolerance matters more than maximum strength.

## 🧴 Build the Foundation First

For two weeks, keep the routine simple: mild cleanser, fragrance-free moisturizer and daytime broad-spectrum SPF 30 or higher. Do not start retinol while skin is cracked, sunburned, actively flaring with eczema or rosacea, or stinging from every product.

## 🌙 Start With Less, Less Often

Choose a low-strength over-the-counter retinol. At night, cleanse and let skin dry. Use one pea-sized amount for the whole face, avoiding eyelids, lip corners and nostril creases. Begin once or twice weekly for several weeks before adding another night.

More product does not work faster; it mainly raises irritation risk. Keep exfoliating acids and scrubs off retinol nights.

## 🥪 Use the Moisturizer Sandwich

Apply a thin layer of moisturizer, wait until comfortable, apply retinol, then add moisturizer again. This buffering can reduce irritation and is especially useful around naturally dry areas. It may slightly slow penetration, which is often a worthwhile trade for consistent use.

Mild temporary dryness can occur, but burning, swelling, crusting or persistent peeling means stop and focus on barrier care. Restart only when fully comfortable, at a lower frequency—or choose another ingredient.

## ☀️ Protect Every Morning

Use broad-spectrum, water-resistant SPF 30 or higher, plus shade and protective clothing. Retinol cannot outperform repeated UV damage. Sunscreen also helps prevent irritation-related marks from darkening.

## 🩺 Know When to Ask a Dermatologist

Prescription retinoids are stronger and not automatically better for dry menopause skin. Ask a dermatologist about acne, significant pigment, rosacea, eczema or a prescription plan. Retinoids should not be used during pregnancy; anyone who could become pregnant should discuss safe contraception and alternatives with a clinician.

## ❓ Quick Questions

**Can I use retinol every night?** Some people eventually can, but nightly use is not required for everyone. Your comfortable schedule is the right schedule.

**Can I put it on my neck?** The neck is often more sensitive. Patch test and use less frequently; stop if irritated.

**Is purging always normal?** Irritant rash is not “purging.” Widespread burning and peeling mean the routine is too aggressive.

## ✅ Bottom Line

During menopause, introduce retinol only after the barrier is stable. Use a small amount infrequently, buffer with moisturizer and pair it with daily sun protection. Stopping an intolerable product is good skincare—not failure.
