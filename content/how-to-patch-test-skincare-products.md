A quick dab behind your ear for one evening can miss a delayed reaction. The American Academy of Dermatology recommends testing a new skincare product on a small area **twice daily for seven to ten days** before normal use.

## 1. Choose the Right Test Spot

Use a quarter-sized area where the product will not be washed or rubbed away constantly, such as the underside of your arm or bend of your elbow. Do not test on broken, sunburned, freshly shaved, or already irritated skin.

## 2. Copy Normal Use

Apply the normal amount and frequency. For a leave-on moisturizer, leave it on. For a cleanser, keep it on only as long as you normally would, then rinse. Testing an exfoliant three times daily would not predict safe real-world use—it would simply overexpose the spot.

Immediate burning matters, but allergic contact reactions can appear later. Check for redness, itching, swelling, bumps, blistering, scaling, or persistent tenderness. Photographing the spot in the same lighting can make subtle changes easier to compare.

## 4. Know What a “Pass” Means

No reaction lowers risk; it does not guarantee that your face will tolerate the product. Facial skin may be more reactive, and combining products can change the experience. After a successful patch test, introduce the product to a small facial area or use it less frequently at first.

## 5. Test One New Product at a Time

If you start a cleanser, serum, moisturizer, and sunscreen together, you will not know which one caused trouble. Add one product, allow time to observe it, then consider the next.

## 🚨 If You React

Wash the product off and stop using it. A cool compress or plain petrolatum may soothe mild irritation. Seek medical advice for a persistent or worsening rash; get urgent help for widespread hives, facial swelling, breathing difficulty, or severe blistering. Bring the ingredient list to your appointment.
