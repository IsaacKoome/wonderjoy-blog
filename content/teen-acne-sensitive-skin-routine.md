Acne treatment often causes some dryness, but skincare should not make a teenager dread washing their face. Burning, cracking and persistent redness can signal irritation, a damaged barrier, contact dermatitis or another condition that needs a different plan.

**Sensitive acne-prone skin improves through careful dosing, not stronger punishment.** Start with a stable base and add only one treatment at a time.

## ðŸ§ª Introduce One Acne Active Slowly

Match the treatment to the acne. Salicylic acid may help blackheads and whiteheads; low-strength benzoyl peroxide can help inflamed pimples. Apply two or three times weekly at first, following label ages. Some people use moisturizer before and after a leave-on treatment to buffer dryness.

Patch testing can reveal immediate irritation but cannot guarantee that weeks of use will be comfortable. Keep everything else unchanged while evaluating the new product.

Pause the new product for swelling, hives, blistering, intense burning or a spreading rash. Seek urgent help for breathing difficulty or facial swelling. Ongoing eczema, painful acne or repeated reactions belongs with a clinician rather than endless product experiments.

**Should sensitive teens exfoliate?** Acne medication may already increase turnover. Extra scrubs and peels are often unnecessary.
