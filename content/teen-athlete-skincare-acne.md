Sports do not need to stop because of acne. However, helmets, chin straps, shoulder pads, tight uniforms and backpack straps can trap heat and sweat while rubbing skin. Dermatologists call the resulting pattern **acne mechanica**.

It often begins as tiny rough bumps exactly where equipment presses. Without changes, those bumps can become inflamed pimples.

## 🎒 Before Practice: Reduce Friction

Start with clean, dry skin. Avoid heavy oils or greasy hair products under a helmet. Place clean, soft padding between equipment and skin when the equipment's safety instructions allow it, and choose moisture-wicking fabric under pads.

Protective gear must still fit correctly. Do not loosen a helmet or remove safety padding just to prevent acne.

## 🧼 Keep Gear and Hands Cleaner

Follow the manufacturer's cleaning instructions for helmet liners, chin straps and pads. Wash reusable headbands and uniforms after each use when possible. Do not share towels, makeup or face cloths.

Wipe shared gym equipment before use, then avoid touching your face. Acne is not contagious, but oil and debris from surfaces can contribute to clogged, irritated skin.

## 🚿 After Practice: Change and Rinse

Shower and change out of sweaty clothing as soon as practical. Cleanse with lukewarm water and fingertips; aggressive scrubbing after every session adds more friction. If a shower is unavailable, change clothes, pat sweat with a clean towel and wash when you get home.

For clogged bumps, a salicylic acid wash may help. Introduce it several times weekly, not after every short activity, and stop if skin becomes persistently dry or sore.

## ☀️ Plan for Outdoor Training

Apply broad-spectrum, water-resistant SPF 30 or higher before outdoor practice and reapply according to the label, especially with sweat. A non-comedogenic formula is less likely to clog acne-prone skin. Add a hat or shade when compatible with the sport and safety rules.

## 🩺 Check Bumps That Are Not Acne

Itchy uniform bumps, pus-filled spots around hairs or a spreading rash may be folliculitis, heat rash, contact dermatitis or infection. Wrestlers and contact-sport athletes should report suspicious ring-shaped, crusted or rapidly spreading lesions promptly and follow team health rules.

See a dermatologist if friction bumps do not improve after six to eight weeks, become painful or leave scars.

## ❓ Quick Questions

**Does sweat itself cause acne?** Sweat plus friction, heat, products and delayed cleansing can create a breakout-friendly environment.

**Can I use disinfectant wipes on my face?** No. Surface disinfectants are not formulated for skin.

**Should I scrub with a rough towel?** Pat sweat away. Rubbing adds the same irritation you are trying to reduce.

## ✅ Bottom Line

Teen athletes can keep playing while protecting their skin: reduce safe-to-change friction, clean equipment, change promptly, wash gently and use non-comedogenic sunscreen outdoors.
