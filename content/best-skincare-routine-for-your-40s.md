Skin often becomes drier and slower to recover in the 40s, while fine lines and sun-related discoloration become more visible. The answer is not to attack every concern at once. A supportive routine reduces irritation and makes targeted products easier to tolerate.

## ☀️ Morning: Hydrate and Defend

### 1. Wash gently

Choose a creamy, fragrance-free cleanser, or simply rinse if your skin is dry on waking. Hot water and rough washcloths can strip the oils your skin has less of now.

### 2. Use an antioxidant if tolerated

Vitamin C may help uneven tone and supports protection against environmental damage, but it does not replace sunscreen. Start every other morning if you are sensitive.

### 4. Apply sunscreen generously

Broad-spectrum SPF 30+ protects collagen from further UV damage and helps prevent uneven pigmentation from worsening. Add sunglasses and shade rather than relying on sunscreen alone.

## 🌙 Night: Target, Then Cushion

Remove makeup and sunscreen with gentle cleanser. Retinol or a prescribed retinoid can improve mild wrinkles, texture, and discoloration, but dryness is common at first. Use a pea-sized amount twice weekly, followed by moisturizer. If needed, moisturize before and after it.

## 🛑 Do Not Chase Peeling

Burning and persistent redness are warning signs, not proof that a product works. Pause actives, simplify the routine, and restart more slowly only after recovery. Avoid retinoids during pregnancy.

## ⏳ Keep Expectations Realistic

Creams can hydrate, smooth texture, and make modest changes over months. They cannot reproduce a facelift, filler, or laser procedure. For deeper wrinkles or rapidly changing spots, see a board-certified dermatologist.

## 🔄 Adjust for Dryness and Sensitivity

Hormonal changes, weather, and medications can alter how skin behaves. If a routine that once felt comfortable suddenly burns, do not force it. Pause retinol and vitamin C, use a bland moisturizer, and restart one product at a time after the skin settles. A dermatologist can assess persistent itching, scaling, or flushing, which may be a skin condition rather than ordinary aging.
