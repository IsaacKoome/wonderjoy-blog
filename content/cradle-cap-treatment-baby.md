Cradle cap is the everyday name for infantile seborrheic dermatitis on the scalp. It can look like greasy yellow scale, dry white flakes or thicker crusts. On darker skin, the skin underneath may look lighter or darker rather than bright red.

It is common, not contagious and not caused by poor hygiene. It also usually does not itch. If the baby seems very itchy or painful, the diagnosis may be something else.

## 🧠 First, Set Realistic Expectations

HealthyChildren.org reports that cradle cap commonly begins in the first months of life and may appear on the face, behind the ears, in neck folds or in the diaper area as seborrheic dermatitis. Many cases improve over weeks or months.

The goal is not to remove every flake in one session. Aggressive scraping can injure the scalp and create an entry point for infection.

## 🫧 Step 1: Wash With a Mild Shampoo

Wet the scalp with warm water and gently massage a mild baby shampoo using fingertips. Rinse thoroughly while supporting the baby's head. Shampooing every few days may be enough; follow the pediatrician's advice if scale builds quickly.

Do not substitute adult dandruff shampoo, salicylic acid, essential oils or a medicated antifungal unless the baby's clinician specifies the product and schedule.

## 🪮 Step 2: Loosen, Never Pick

After washing, use a very soft baby brush to move only scales that release easily. Stop if the skin becomes red, tender or starts bleeding. Do not use fingernails, tweezers or a fine comb to lift attached crust.

For stubborn scale, some clinicians advise briefly softening it with a small amount of mineral oil before shampooing. Ask first, use very little and wash it out thoroughly; leaving heavy oil can worsen buildup or heat rash.

## 🚫 Common Mistakes

- Picking at attached flakes
- Applying fragranced hair oils
- Leaving thick oil on the scalp all day
- Using adult medicated shampoo without instructions
- Assuming every scaly rash is cradle cap

Cradle cap and eczema can overlap, but eczema is often much itchier. Ringworm, psoriasis and infection can also cause scalp scale and need different treatment.

## 🚩 When to Call the Pediatrician

Arrange care if home care is not helping, scale spreads widely, the scalp is very inflamed, the baby appears itchy or uncomfortable, hair is breaking or falling out in patches, or you see swelling, pus, odor, bleeding or fever. A clinician may recommend a medicated shampoo or lotion after confirming the cause.

## ❓ Quick Questions

**Does cradle cap mean the scalp is dirty?** No. Washing too aggressively can make the skin sore.

**Can it spread to the face?** Seborrheic dermatitis can affect eyebrows, ears and folds. Have widespread or irritated areas checked.

**Should I moisturize the scalp every day?** Not automatically. Heavy leave-on products can trap buildup; ask which method fits the baby's scalp.

## ✅ Bottom Line

Use mild washing and a soft brush, remove only loose scale and stop before the scalp becomes irritated. Persistent or spreading cradle cap deserves pediatric review.
