The flat red, pink, purple, or brown spots left after a pimple are often called acne scars, but many are **post-acne marks**, not permanent texture changes.

Brown or gray-brown marks are commonly post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH). Red, pink, or purple marks are often described as post-inflammatory erythema (PIE). Both can fade, but repeated irritation and new acne keep resetting the clock.

---

## Step 1: Prevent New Marks

Treat active acne early and stop picking. Squeezing increases inflammation, which raises the chance of discoloration and true scarring.

Choose one acne active suited to your breakouts and use it consistently. More products do not mean faster healing.

## Step 2: Wear Sunscreen Every Day

UV and visible light can make pigmentation more persistent. Use broad-spectrum SPF 30 or higher each morning and reapply during extended outdoor exposure.

Tinted sunscreen containing iron oxides may offer additional visible-light protection for people prone to hyperpigmentation.

## Step 3: Add One Brightening Ingredient

Helpful options include:

- **Azelaic acid:** can treat acne and post-acne discoloration
- **Niacinamide:** supports the barrier and can improve uneven tone
- **Vitamin C:** may help brighten uneven pigmentation
- **Retinoids:** can unclog pores and gradually improve dark marks

Introduce one product slowly. If your skin burns, peels heavily, or stays red, pause and simplify.

## A Simple Routine

### Morning

1. Gentle cleanser
2. Brightening serum
3. Moisturizer
4. Sunscreen

### Night

1. Gentle cleanser
2. Acne treatment or retinoid on scheduled nights
3. Moisturizer

Do not combine every acid and serum in one routine. Irritation can trigger more discoloration, especially in deeper skin tones.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can exfoliating every day fade marks faster?

Usually not. Frequent acids can create inflammation, peeling, and barrier damage—the exact conditions that make discoloration look worse. Use the frequency on the label and build tolerance slowly.

### Do red marks and brown marks need different care?

They arise from different visible changes, but both benefit from preventing new acne, avoiding picking, and daily sun protection. Brown PIH often responds to pigment-targeting ingredients. Persistent redness may need a dermatologist to confirm the diagnosis and discuss options.

### When is a mark actually a scar?

Look at texture in side lighting. A completely flat color change is a mark; a depression, pit, or raised firm area is a scar. Topical brighteners can improve color but cannot rebuild an indented scar.

## How Long Will It Take?

Some marks improve over several months; deeper discoloration can take longer. Indented or raised scars need different treatment. See a dermatologist if marks are not improving, acne is still active, or you notice changes in skin texture.
