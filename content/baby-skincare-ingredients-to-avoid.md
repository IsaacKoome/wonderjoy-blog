Baby products do not become gentle merely because the label says “natural,” “clean,” “hypoallergenic” or “pediatrician tested.” The most useful rule is **use fewer products, choose fragrance-free formulas and avoid adult active ingredients unless the baby's clinician prescribed them.**

This guide is about reducing avoidable exposure, not declaring every unfamiliar ingredient dangerous.

## 🌸 Fragrance and Perfume

Fragrance can irritate skin or trigger allergic contact dermatitis. “Unscented” may still contain masking fragrance, so look for “fragrance-free” and read the complete ingredient list. Fragrance can appear in wash, lotion, wipes, detergent and fabric softener.

Stop a new product if redness repeatedly appears where it touches and bring the label to the pediatrician.

## 🌿 Essential Oils and Complex Botanicals

Lavender, tea tree, citrus, peppermint and eucalyptus oils are not required for clean skin. They can irritate or cause allergy, and concentrated oil creates dosing and ingestion concerns around hands and mouths.

Edible does not mean suitable for skin. Avoid homemade mixtures whose concentration, contamination and stability are unknown.

## 🧂 Talc, Cornstarch and Loose Powder

Powder can become airborne and be inhaled. It may also clump in damp folds rather than prevent irritation. For diaper rash, AAP guidance favors frequent changes, gentle cleaning and an appropriate zinc oxide or petrolatum barrier—not clouds of powder.

## 🧪 Adult Acne, Exfoliating and Anti-Aging Actives

Do not apply retinoids, salicylic or glycolic acid, benzoyl peroxide, hydroquinone, peeling solutions or scrubs to baby acne, milia or rough patches. Typical baby acne and milia often resolve without treatment. A persistent rash needs diagnosis, not an adult serum.

## 💊 Antibiotic, Steroid and Numbing Creams

Medication should match a confirmed problem. Topical antibiotic ingredients can cause contact allergy; steroid strength and duration matter more in babies; numbing ingredients are not ordinary rash care. Use only what a clinician recommends for that baby and body area.

## ✅ What a Short Label Routine Looks Like

Most babies need water, a mild fragrance-free cleanser when soiled, and a fragrance-free cream or ointment when dry. Add an appropriate diaper barrier and age-specific sun protection as needed. Introduce one product at a time so a reaction is easier to trace.

## 🚩 When a Product Reaction Needs Help

Wash off the product and call for persistent or worsening redness. Seek urgent care for widespread hives, facial swelling, trouble breathing, blistering, fever, pus, severe pain or a baby who seems unwell. Bring the package or a label photo.

## ❓ Quick Questions

**Is “hypoallergenic” a guarantee?** No. Any product can irritate or cause allergy in an individual child.

**Do babies need separate face and body lotion?** Usually not. One suitable fragrance-free moisturizer can keep the routine simpler.

**Should I patch test?** Ask the pediatrician for a baby-appropriate introduction plan, especially with eczema or previous reactions.

## ✅ Bottom Line

Avoid unnecessary fragrance, essential oils, powder and adult actives. A short routine makes reactions easier to prevent, recognize and explain to the pediatrician.
