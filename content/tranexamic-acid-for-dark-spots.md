Tranexamic acid began as a medication used to control bleeding, but topical versions now appear in many discoloration serums. Research suggests it may help some forms of excess pigmentation by interrupting signals involved in melanin production. That makes it interesting for **post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation and melasma**—but not an overnight spot eraser.

Topical skincare is also different from oral tranexamic acid. Oral treatment has important medical risks and should never be started from beauty advice online.

## 🔬 How Topical Tranexamic Acid May Work

Inflammation and ultraviolet exposure activate pathways that encourage pigment production. Tranexamic acid appears to reduce plasmin-related signaling between skin cells and melanocytes. In plain language, it may turn down one of the messages telling skin to make extra pigment.

A systematic review of post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation found encouraging results, but only nine studies and 196 participants met its criteria. Products also differed in concentration and delivery method. The evidence is promising rather than final.

## 🎯 What Can It Treat?

Topical tranexamic acid is most often used for flat brown or gray-brown discoloration, including marks after acne and patches associated with melasma. It cannot fill indented acne scars or remove raised scars. A changing mole or unexplained dark patch needs medical assessment, not a brightening serum.

Expect gradual change over **eight to twelve weeks**, sometimes longer. Pigment located deeper in the skin tends to respond more slowly.

## 🧴 How to Add It to Your Routine

Patch-test first. After cleansing, apply a thin layer to dry skin, then moisturizer. Start every other day if the formula contains several active ingredients. If comfortable, follow its label for daily use.

Morning:

1. Gentle cleanser or water rinse
2. Tranexamic-acid serum
3. Moisturizer if needed
4. Broad-spectrum SPF 30+

Evening can repeat the serum or reserve it for alternate nights. Do not introduce it alongside a new retinoid, exfoliating acid and vitamin C all at once; irritation can create more pigmentation.

## ☀️ Sunscreen Is Part of the Treatment

Without sun protection, UV and visible light can keep pigment active. Use broad-spectrum SPF 30 or higher every morning. For melasma or persistent dark spots—especially on deeper skin tones—consider a tinted sunscreen containing iron oxides for additional visible-light protection.

## ⚠️ Safety and Limitations

Topical products may cause dryness, stinging or redness. Stop for swelling, blistering or persistent irritation. Pregnancy and breastfeeding data for cosmetic topical use are limited, so ask your clinician.

Oral tranexamic acid can increase clot-related risk in susceptible people and belongs under physician supervision. A topical bottle is not permission to obtain tablets independently.

## ❓ Quick Questions

**Can I use it with niacinamide?** Many formulas combine them. Introduce the complete product slowly.

**Is it better than vitamin C?** They work differently, and direct comparisons are limited. Choose one consistent routine before layering both.

**When should I see a dermatologist?** For melasma, rapidly changing pigment, irritation, or no improvement after three careful months.

## ✅ Bottom Line

Topical tranexamic acid is a promising option for dark marks and melasma, but evidence is still developing. Pair patient use with sunscreen and avoid aggressive combinations. Consistent photographs taken in the same light are more reliable than judging subtle pigment changes from one day to the next.
