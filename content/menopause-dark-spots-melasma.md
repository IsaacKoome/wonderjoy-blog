Brown patches can become more noticeable around menopause, but not every mark has the same cause. Years of sun exposure may produce age spots; acne or irritation can leave post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation; melasma tends to form broader, often symmetrical patches.

**Do not diagnose a new or changing spot from a photo.** Begin with sun protection and gentle care, then let a dermatologist assess anything unusual.

## ☀️ Make Daily Protection the Treatment Base

Choose broad-spectrum, water-resistant SPF 30 or higher. For melasma or stubborn hyperpigmentation, the American Academy of Dermatology recommends considering a tinted formula containing iron oxide because visible light can worsen discoloration, especially in darker skin tones.

Apply enough to all exposed skin, then reapply outdoors as the label directs. Add shade and a wide-brimmed hat.

## 🌿 Treat Without Creating New Pigment

Irritation itself can darken marks, particularly in medium to deep skin tones. Start with one product, patch test and introduce it two or three nights weekly. Azelaic acid can help acne and uneven tone; niacinamide is another gentle supportive option. Prescription hydroquinone, tretinoin and combination creams require clinician guidance.

If a product burns or causes prolonged peeling, stop. More inflammation is not faster fading.

## 💧 Keep the Barrier Comfortable

Use a mild fragrance-free cleanser and moisturizer. Apply treatment to dry skin, then moisturize unless its directions say otherwise. Avoid grainy scrubs, lemon juice and unregulated bleaching mixtures; these can burn skin or contain unsafe ingredients.

## ⏳ Use a Realistic Timeline

Some surface dark spots take six to twelve months to fade once the trigger stops; deeper pigment can take longer. Melasma can persist or recur and usually needs ongoing sun protection. Take monthly photos in the same natural light rather than inspecting daily.

Procedures such as peels and lasers can help selected patients but can also worsen pigment when poorly chosen. See a dermatologist experienced with your skin tone.

## 🚩 Have These Spots Examined

Book a skin check for a new, changing, itchy, bleeding or non-healing spot, or one that looks different from your others. An “age spot” should not be assumed harmless without assessment when it changes.

## ❓ Quick Questions

**Does menopause cause melasma?** Hormones can influence melasma, but sun and visible light are major triggers and individual patterns vary.

**Can I use several brighteners together?** Starting multiple actives raises irritation risk. Add one at a time.

**Will sunscreen fade spots by itself?** It helps prevent worsening and supports fading, but established pigment may need additional treatment.

## ✅ Bottom Line

A dark-spot plan during menopause should be patient and non-irritating: tinted broad-spectrum sunscreen, one suitable treatment and steady barrier care. Get changing spots examined before calling them cosmetic.
