Pregnancy can change oil production, sensitivity and pigmentation, but it does not require a ten-step routine. A useful foundation is **gentle cleansing, moisturizer and broad-spectrum SPF 30+**, with only one targeted product when needed.

“Pregnancy-safe” is not a universal certification. Formulas change, concentration and application area matter, and prescriptions need individual review. Show your obstetrician or dermatologist the complete ingredient lists—especially if you are trying to conceive, pregnant or breastfeeding.

## 🧾 First: Audit What You Already Use

Check active ingredients rather than relying on the front label. ACOG and the American Academy of Dermatology recommend avoiding retinoids during pregnancy, including prescription tretinoin and over-the-counter retinol or adapalene. Do not abruptly stop an essential prescription without contacting its prescriber.

Remove unnecessary scrubs, peels and overlapping serums. Pregnancy can make previously comfortable products sting, and a short routine makes the cause easier to identify.

## 🌅 Morning Step 1: Cleanse Gently

Wash with lukewarm water and a mild fragrance-free cleanser, using fingertips rather than a brush. Once daily cleansing may be enough for dry skin; acne-prone skin can usually cleanse up to twice daily and after sweating.

Apply a fragrance-free moisturizer to slightly damp skin. Humectants such as glycerin draw water into the outer layer, while ceramides and occlusive ingredients help reduce water loss. Choose a lotion for oilier skin or a cream for persistent dryness.

## ☀️ Morning Step 3: Protect Against UV

Use shade, a hat and broad-spectrum, water-resistant SPF 30 or higher. The AAD suggests a physical sunscreen containing zinc oxide, titanium dioxide or both during pregnancy, particularly when irritation is a concern. Apply enough and reapply after two hours outdoors, swimming or sweating.

## 🌙 Evening: Cleanse, Treat Only if Needed, Moisturize

Remove makeup and sunscreen without scrubbing. For acne or discoloration, ACOG lists topical azelaic acid, benzoyl peroxide, salicylic acid and glycolic acid among over-the-counter options that can be used during pregnancy. That does not mean use all four. Confirm your choice, start slowly and avoid applying to broken or highly irritated skin.

Patch test one product for seven to ten days. Stop if you develop swelling, hives, blistering or a worsening rash.

## 🚩 When a Skin Change Needs Care

Call your maternity clinician promptly for severe itching without a rash—especially on palms or soles—new blisters, rapidly spreading rash, facial swelling, fever, pus or eye involvement. Not every pregnancy-related skin change belongs in a cosmetic routine.

## ❓ Quick Questions

**Is “natural skincare” automatically safer in pregnancy?** No. Essential oils, botanical extracts and fragrance can irritate or cause allergy, and “natural” does not describe pregnancy evidence.

**Do I need to replace every product?** Usually not. Keep simple products whose complete ingredients your care team accepts, and replace only unsuitable or irritating items.

**Can the same routine continue while breastfeeding?** Ask again. Pregnancy and breastfeeding guidance can differ by ingredient, application area and possible infant contact.

## ✅ Bottom Line

The best pregnancy routine is deliberately simple: gentle cleanser, suitable moisturizer and consistent SPF. Review medications and active ingredients with your care team, then add only what addresses a real concern.
