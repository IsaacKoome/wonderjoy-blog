Dark marks on the legs, arms, chest, back, or bikini line often remain after inflammation heals. This is called **post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH)**: the skin produces extra pigment in response to a pimple, ingrown hair, bite, rash, burn, or repeated friction.

PIH is especially noticeable in medium-to-deep skin tones, but it can affect anyone. Safe treatment aims to return skin toward its natural tone—not bleach normal surrounding skin.

## 1. Stop the Trigger Before Treating the Mark

If acne, eczema, folliculitis, shaving irritation, or scratching keeps producing inflammation, new marks will replace the ones that fade. Identify the pattern: Does it follow workouts? Hair removal? A fragranced lotion? Tight clothing? Treating the active cause is step one.

Persistent itch, scaling, recurrent infection, or unexplained discoloration needs professional assessment. Lightening a mark without identifying a rash underneath can delay appropriate care.

## 2. Protect Areas Exposed to Light

UV—and, for some pigment conditions, visible light—can deepen discoloration. Apply broad-spectrum SPF 30 or higher to exposed arms, legs, chest, shoulders, and back. Clothing and shade add useful protection. Sunscreen is not needed under opaque clothing, but thin or wet fabric may offer less coverage.

## 3. Choose One Fading Ingredient

Options used for uneven pigment include azelaic acid, retinoids, vitamin C, niacinamide, glycolic acid, and other ingredients. Body skin may be thicker than facial skin, but it can still become irritated. More acids do not equal faster fading.

Patch test a product, begin on a small area, and follow the label. Azelaic acid may be useful when pigment follows acne or follicular bumps because it can address both discoloration and inflammation for some users.

## 4. Moisturize to Reduce Irritation

Apply fragrance-free moisturizer after bathing, especially if you use an exfoliating or brightening product. A supported barrier tolerates treatment better. If a product burns, causes a rash, or makes marks darker, stop and let skin recover.

## 5. Avoid Dangerous “Bleaching” Shortcuts

Never apply household bleach. Be cautious with unlabeled lightening creams, imported products from unknown sellers, and products that promise whole-body whitening. Some have contained undisclosed steroids or mercury. A dermatologist can recommend regulated treatments for stubborn pigment.

## ⏳ How Long Do Body Dark Marks Take to Fade?

The AAD notes that a spot only a few shades darker than your natural skin color may take **six to twelve months** to fade once its cause stops. Deeper pigment can take longer. Photos in consistent lighting every four weeks are more useful than daily mirror checks.

## ❓ Quick Questions

**Can I scrub pigment away?** No. Pigment is not surface dirt, and friction can trigger more PIH.

**Why is my bikini line getting darker?** Shaving, ingrowns, rubbing, and irritation are common contributors, but a velvety thick darkening in folds can have other causes and should be assessed.

**When should I see a dermatologist?** Get help for rapidly changing pigment, a suspicious individual spot, unexplained widespread darkening, or marks that persist despite controlling the cause.

## ✅ Bottom Line

Body PIH improves most reliably when you stop new inflammation, protect exposed skin, use one tolerable fading ingredient, and wait months rather than days. Gentle consistency beats aggressive bleaching.
