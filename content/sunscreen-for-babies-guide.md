Sun protection for babies begins with the environment, not a bottle. **For infants younger than six months, the FDA and American Academy of Pediatrics recommend avoiding direct sunlight and prioritizing shade and protective clothing.** Ask the pediatrician before applying sunscreen to this age group.

At six months and older, sunscreen becomes an added layer—not permission for long periods in strong sun.

## 👶 Under 6 Months: Build Shade

Plan walks for cooler hours. Use tree shade, a stroller canopy or umbrella while preserving airflow. Dress the baby in lightweight long sleeves, trousers and a brimmed hat when appropriate for the temperature.

Do not drape a thick blanket over the stroller; it can trap heat. Check the baby's neck and chest for overheating and move indoors when shade is inadequate.

If small areas cannot be covered, ask the pediatrician whether limited sunscreen application is appropriate. This is especially important for premature babies or infants with eczema or another skin condition.

## ☀️ At 6 Months: Add Broad-Spectrum SPF 30+

Choose broad-spectrum, water-resistant SPF 30 or higher. Mineral formulas containing zinc oxide, titanium dioxide or both are a practical starting point for many sensitive children, but fragrance and other inactive ingredients still matter.

Patch test a small area before the first full use. Apply before going outdoors to exposed skin, including ears and tops of feet, while avoiding eyes and mouth. Reapply at least every two hours outdoors and after swimming, sweating or towel drying.

## 🧪 Patch Test Without Creating False Certainty

Test on a small patch of intact skin on a day when you can observe the child. A comfortable patch does not guarantee no later allergy, but it can reveal immediate stinging or irritation before a large application.

Wash off a product that causes persistent burning, swelling or rash. Do not keep trialing multiple sunscreens on inflamed skin; ask the pediatrician or dermatologist for alternatives.

## 🛁 Remove, Then Moisturize if Needed

At the end of the day, wash sunscreen away with warm water and a mild fragrance-free cleanser. Pat dry and apply a simple moisturizer if the skin feels dry. Do not scrub away mineral residue.

## 🚩 Sunburn Needs Attention

Move a sunburned baby out of the sun and contact the pediatrician, particularly for an infant, blistering, fever, significant pain, unusual sleepiness or poor feeding. Do not apply numbing creams, ice or home remedies unless advised.

## ❓ Quick Questions

**Is SPF 50 twice as protective as SPF 25?** No. Higher SPF adds protection but does not replace enough product, coverage or reapplication.

**Can sunscreen prevent overheating?** No. Sunscreen reduces UV injury; shade, airflow, timing and clothing manage heat exposure.

**Is spray sunscreen easier?** Lotion makes coverage easier to see and avoids inhalation. If using spray later in childhood, never spray directly toward the face.

## ✅ Bottom Line

Under six months, lead with shade and clothing and ask before sunscreen. From six months, add a tolerated SPF 30+ while keeping every physical protection habit.
