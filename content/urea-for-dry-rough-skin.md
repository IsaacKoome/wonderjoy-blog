The word “urea” may sound unusual on a moisturizer label, but urea already exists naturally in the outer skin as part of its natural moisturizing factor. In topical products, it can attract water, support barrier function and—at higher concentrations—help loosen thick, rough scale.

That concentration difference is important. A gentle face moisturizer and a strong heel cream are not interchangeable simply because both contain urea.

## 💧 Lower Strengths Focus on Hydration

Products around **2% to 10% urea** are generally used as moisturizers. Urea is hygroscopic, meaning it helps bind water in the stratum corneum. Reviews also describe effects on skin-cell differentiation and barrier-related antimicrobial defenses.

Lower-strength formulas may suit ordinary dry skin, flaky legs and rough-feeling arms. The finished formula still matters: humectants attract water, while emollients smooth gaps and occlusive ingredients slow evaporation.

## 🦶 Higher Strengths Soften Thick Skin

At concentrations above roughly 10%, urea becomes increasingly keratolytic: it helps soften and separate built-up keratin. Stronger products are often designed for heels, calluses and very scaly areas.

Higher does not mean better for every location. A 20% or 40% foot cream may burn on the face, freshly shaved legs, eczema cracks or broken skin. Follow the specific product label and ask a clinician before treating an undiagnosed thick patch.

## 🧴 How to Use It

Apply after a short lukewarm shower while skin is slightly damp. Start once daily on a small area. For ordinary dryness, use the product as the moisturizing step; for a strong targeted heel product, keep it only on the thick area and wash hands afterward.

Do not combine high-strength urea immediately with scrubs, pumice, exfoliating acids or retinoids on the same skin. Over-softening and friction can create rawness rather than smoothness.

Ordinary dryness may feel better within days, while thick heel scale takes longer. Reapply consistently rather than peeling softened skin by hand. Shoes that rub, very hot showers and low indoor humidity can keep recreating roughness even when the cream itself is appropriate.

## ⚠️ Why Urea Sometimes Stings

Stinging is more likely at higher concentrations and on inflamed, fissured or recently exfoliated skin. Mild brief tingling may occur, but persistent burning, redness or swelling means stop. Patch-test if you have sensitive skin.

Dryness with deep cracks, bleeding, intense itching, pain or signs of infection needs medical care. Diabetes, poor circulation or reduced foot sensation also make self-treating calluses risky.

## ☀️ Protect Exposed Areas

Urea itself is not a substitute for sunscreen. When rough or pigmented areas are exposed, use broad-spectrum SPF 30+ and protective clothing. This is especially important when another exfoliating ingredient is part of the routine.

## ❓ Quick Questions

**Is urea made from urine?** Cosmetic urea is manufactured synthetically; it is not collected from urine.

**Can it help keratosis pilaris?** Its moisturizing and keratolytic actions may soften rough bumps, but begin with a suitable strength and avoid scrubbing.

**Can I use it daily?** Lower-strength moisturizers often can be used daily as labeled. Strong foot treatments need more targeted directions.

## ✅ Bottom Line

Urea is both a humectant and, at higher strengths, a scale-softening ingredient. Match concentration to the body area and stop if stinging becomes persistent.
