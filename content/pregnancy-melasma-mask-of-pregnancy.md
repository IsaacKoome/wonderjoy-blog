Melasma causes flat brown or gray-brown patches, often on the cheeks, forehead, nose or upper lip. Pregnancy can trigger it, which is why it is sometimes called the “mask of pregnancy.” It is harmless, but it can resemble other causes of pigmentation, so diagnosis matters before treatment.

ACOG says pregnancy-related dark patches often fade after childbirth, although some persist for years. The most useful first step is not aggressive bleaching—it is preventing light and irritation from continually stimulating pigment.

## 🔍 Confirm That It Is Melasma

Melasma is commonly symmetrical and does not scale, hurt or form a lump. A new changing mole, one-sided patch, itchy rash, bleeding spot or rapidly changing pigment needs examination rather than a brightening serum.

Take photographs in consistent natural light. Hormonal changes, sun exposure and some medicines can influence melasma, but the exact contribution differs between people.

## ☀️ Make Light Protection the Main Treatment

Use broad-spectrum, water-resistant SPF 30+ every morning, plus a wide-brimmed hat and shade. Apply enough to cover the face and neck, and reapply during prolonged outdoor exposure.

Tinted sunscreen containing iron oxides may help protect against visible light, which can worsen melasma, while matching the tint can reduce white cast. During pregnancy, the AAD suggests physical filters such as zinc oxide or titanium dioxide to minimize irritation.

## 🌙 Keep Evening Care Conservative

Cleanse gently and moisturize. Ask whether azelaic acid or glycolic acid suits your pregnancy and skin. Avoid combining several exfoliants. Irritation can make pigmentation harder to manage, particularly in deeper skin tones.

Retinoids should be avoided during pregnancy. Do not use prescription hydroquinone, combination melasma creams, peels or lasers without a dermatologist who knows you are pregnant.

## 💧 Reduce Irritation With Moisturizer

A plain moisturizer supports tolerance of sunscreen and any clinician-approved active. Fragrance and essential oils are not melasma treatments and can provoke dermatitis.

## ⏳ What Happens After Delivery?

Some melasma fades as pregnancy-related hormonal changes settle; some persists. If patches remain, a dermatologist can confirm the diagnosis and discuss postpartum treatment, considering breastfeeding and future pregnancy plans.

## ❓ Quick Questions

**Does melasma mean something is wrong with the pregnancy?** Melasma itself is a common pigment change, but an unusual or changing spot still deserves diagnosis.

**Can foundation replace tinted sunscreen?** Makeup may contain pigment but usually is not applied in the amount required for labeled sun protection. Use sunscreen first.

**Should I exfoliate the patches every day?** No. Over-exfoliation can inflame skin and deepen uneven tone. Treatment should be gradual and low irritation.

## ✅ Bottom Line

Pregnancy melasma is managed first with disciplined light protection and low-irritation skincare. Expect gradual change, avoid aggressive self-treatment and arrange diagnosis if the pattern is unusual.
